Mail Art Documentation

Roads I Walk

A simple interactive archive of my mail art piece. This work documents 1230 N LBJ Drive through memory, movement, collected materials, and hand-drawn illustrations.

Scroll to read

The main purpose of this page is to represent the front, middle, and back of the mail art piece as a readable visual journey. Rather than showing only the final object, it also explains the materials, symbols, and decisions used in the work.

Front / Familiar Road
Part One

The Road I Became Familiar With

The front side focuses on 1230 N LBJ Drive, a road that became repeated, known, and personally meaningful through everyday movement. Instead of showing the road as one fixed image, I used different moments, different views, and different modes of presence to suggest how a place slowly becomes part of memory.

The images are not only visual records. I wanted the front to feel like an observation like a road seen again and again, but never in exactly the same way.

Small elements collected from the site, including rocks and china berries, are attached as references to what physically exists there. These materials are not added as decoration. They act as evidence, texture, and memory.

Middle / Route + Motion
Part Two

Map, Movement, and Journey

The middle section works like a transition. It shifts from collected fragments to a more direct idea of movement through space. A redmarked route on the map identifies the N LBJ DR road.

The small 3D pop up car brings in the feeling of the road as something active and lived. It suggests motion and travel kind of vibe that one is prensent there.

The illustrated trees are drawn as the appearence of them in road and placed around the route to hold the atmosphere of the surroundings. They are important because the memory of this road is not only the pavement or address. It is also shaped by the trees, shadows, and small details that frame the experience.

Back / Surrounding Memory
Part Three

The Memory of What Surrounds the Road

The back section holds the quieter memory of the place. Here, the focus moves to the surrounding natural elements — sycamore leaves,elm leaves, privet leaves, stones and other traces gathered from the environment.

These materials matter because they represent what stays around the road even when the road itself is no longer in front of me.

In this part of the work, the road becomes a memory field. The back preserves the physical presence of the surroundings and turns the mail art into a document of attachment, and surroundings.

The Exchange Continues

My Response: Between Here and Home

My Response / Distance & Home

This piece is my response to the mail art my friends sent me. Our shared theme was distance and the feeling of missing home while living far away. I wanted to show that distance is not measured only in miles. It can also be felt through people, food, familiar places, achievements, and the small memories we continue to carry.

The collage places Bangladesh and the United States within the same visual space. Maps, flags, stamps, postmarks, travel marks, personal milestones, and everyday memories sit beside one another. Together, they show what it feels like to build a life in one place while still feeling deeply connected to another.

Sending this response back completes the exchange. The project becomes a two-way conversation in which mail art carries memory across distance and creates a connection between the place I live now and the country I still call home.
